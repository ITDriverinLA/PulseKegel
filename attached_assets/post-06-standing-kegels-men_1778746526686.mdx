# Standing Kegels for Men: The Progression That Builds Real-World Control

If you only do Kegels lying down, you might be training a skill that stays on the floor.

In real life, your pelvic floor has to work while you are upright: walking, carrying, lifting, laughing, coughing, and yes—during sexual activity.

That is why “standing Kegels” are useful. Not because standing is harder for the sake of hard, but because it is closer to how you actually use the muscle.

This guide shows you how to progress from lying → sitting → standing without bracing, over-squeezing, or turning every rep into a max-effort clench.

## The Big Idea: Train Positions, Not Just Reps

Reputable pelvic floor guidance is surprisingly consistent:

- Start in a position where you can feel the muscle clearly (often lying down).
- Practice a clean squeeze, a steady hold, and a full release.
- Then progress to sitting and standing as control improves.

The goal is transfer: the same clean contraction and full release, even when gravity and daily movement add challenge.

## Quick Form Check (So Standing Kegels Actually Work)

Before you go upright, get these basics right:

- **Breathe.** If you cannot breathe normally, the rep is too hard.
- **No “whole-body squeeze.”** Keep glutes, thighs, and abs as quiet as possible.
- **Full release matters.** A “strong” pelvic floor is not just a strong squeeze—it is also a controlled return to neutral.

If standing makes you tense everywhere, step back to sitting until the signal is clean.

## The 3-Position Progression (Lying → Sitting → Standing)

Use the same routine in three positions. Keep effort around **50–70%**, not 100%.

### Step 1: Lying Down (Teach the Signal)

Best for: learning what a clean contraction feels like.

- Lie on your back, knees bent.
- Inhale normally.
- On the exhale, gently lift the pelvic floor (think “lift and close,” not “crush and clench”).
- Release fully on the next inhale.

Do not worry about intensity yet. Nail the skill.

### Step 2: Sitting (Build Steady Endurance)

Best for: staying relaxed while holding.

- Sit tall, shoulders relaxed.
- Do the same gentle lift on the exhale.
- Keep your jaw and glutes loose.

If you feel your abs bracing, reduce effort and shorten the hold.

### Step 3: Standing (Train Real-World Control)

Best for: carryover to daily life and athletic movement.

- Stand tall with soft knees.
- Keep ribs stacked over hips (avoid “military posture” bracing).
- Exhale gently and lift the pelvic floor.
- Release fully.

If standing makes you grip harder, your “next progression” is not more reps—it is *cleaner reps*.

## A Simple 5-Minute Routine You Can Repeat

You can do this once per day for 7 days.

### Minute 1: Breath setup

- Inhale 4 seconds
- Exhale 6 seconds
- Keep shoulders and glutes relaxed

### Minute 2: Lying reps (signal)

- 6 reps: **hold 3 seconds**, **release 5 seconds**

### Minute 3: Sitting reps (steady)

- 6 reps: **hold 3 seconds**, **release 5 seconds**

### Minute 4: Standing reps (transfer)

- 6 reps: **hold 2 seconds**, **release 6 seconds**

Shorter holds + longer releases is often the cleanest way to stay relaxed upright.

### Minute 5: Reset (do nothing)

- Slow breathing only
- Let the pelvic floor drop back to neutral

If you finish and feel tighter, not calmer: cut the volume in half next time.

## For Lifters: How to Use the Skill (Without Turning It Into a Brace)

Some guidance suggests lightly engaging the pelvic floor *just before and during* activities that raise pressure (like coughing or heavy lifting).

The key word is **lightly**.

Try this:

- Before a hard effort: exhale slightly and do a *small* pelvic floor lift.
- Keep breathing (no “everything clenched” strategy).
- After the effort: release back to neutral.

If you are consistently bearing down (pressure going *down*), treat that as a form error and reduce intensity until you can keep the lift subtle.

## The Mistakes That Make Standing Kegels Useless

### Mistake 1: Practicing by stopping urine every time

Stopping urine midstream can help you *find* the muscle once in a while, but doing Kegels during urination as a routine is commonly discouraged because it can interfere with normal bladder emptying and may increase infection risk.

### Mistake 2: Chasing high reps

“Hundreds a day” is not a flex. High volume with poor release often just trains tension.

### Mistake 3: Forgetting the release

If you only practice contraction, you train one half of control.

Release is the rep you will need most when it counts.

## FAQ

### Should men do Kegels standing up?

Many men can benefit from progressing to standing once they can feel a clean contraction and full release lying down or sitting.

### How many standing Kegels should I do?

Start small. A few clean reps with full release is better than more reps that turn into bracing. Use the routine above as a simple baseline.

### How soon should I expect results?

Progress is usually measured in weeks, not days. Consistency and form matter more than intensity.
